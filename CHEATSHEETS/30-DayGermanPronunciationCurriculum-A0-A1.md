# 30-Day German Pronunciation Curriculum (ADHD-Friendly, A0-A1)

**Overview:** This 30-day plan is structured into 4 weeks (plus 2
wrap-up days) of daily 15-minute pronunciation micro-drills, with one
longer hyperfocus session each week and a passive immersion day every
Sunday. The routine is designed for adult learners with ADHD -- using
short, focused tasks
(Pomodoro-style)[\[1\]](https://sparkl.me/blog/ap/listening-at-speed-mastering-shadowing-and-dictation-drills-for-ap-success/#:~:text=,a%20log%20of%20repeated%20errors),
minimal decision-making, and high-interest native content. You will use
pre-loaded native audio (Forvo for words/phrases, Easy German videos)
for listening practice, and regularly record yourself to compare with
native pronunciations (a proven method to identify and fix
errors[\[2\]](https://talkpal.ai/master-easy-german-pronunciation-speak-like-a-native-fast/#:~:text=5,Speech)[\[3\]](https://storylearning.com/fossilised-errors#:~:text=How%20to%20correct%20fossilised%20mistakes%3F)).
Each week has clear goals and builds on previous skills, targeting
German vowels, umlauts, diphthongs, tricky consonants (like **ch** and
**r**), word/sentence stress, and final devoicing. **Tip:** Print this
plan or use a habit tracker to check off each day. Keep a journal of
problem sounds and improvements -- and save a **baseline** recording on
Day 1 to compare with your Day 30 recording!

## Week 2: Diphthongs & Final Devoicing üéØ

*Goal:* Learn German **diphthongs** (combined vowel sounds like *ei, ie,
au, eu*) and the important **final devoicing** rule for consonants
(B/D/G at word-end). Continue improving overall clarity while practicing
these sounds in words and short sentences.

**Day 8:** *Diphthongs Part 1 -- EI vs IE* -- **Nailing "ei" (like
"eye") vs "ie" (like "ee")**\
- **Hear the Difference:** German diphthongs can be tricky because
English spelling might mislead you. The combo **"ei"** in German is
pronounced like English *"eye"* (long **ƒ´**), whereas **"ie"** is
pronounced like *"ee"* (long
**ƒì**)[\[16\]](https://deutsch-mentor.com/blog/german-pronunciation-practice-exercises#:~:text=mentor,to%20master%20sounds%20and).
Listen to **"mein"** (my) versus **"Miene"** (facial expression) --
*mein* sounds like English "mine" and *Miene* sounds like "mee-nuh".
Another classic pair: **"reisen"** (to travel) vs **"riesen"** (as in
*Riesenrad*, meaning Ferris wheel). Use Forvo to play **"mein"** and
**"Miene"**, or **"Reise"** vs **"Riese"**. - **Repeat with Words:**
Practice a list of *ei* vs *ie* words: **"mein"** (mine) vs **"mies"**
(bad), **"Wein"** (wine) vs **"Wien"** (Vienna), **"Leid"** (sorrow) vs
**"Lied"** (song). Say each pair slowly, really separating the sounds:
*"ma-eyn"* vs *"meed"*. Exaggerate if needed (e.g., "Wien" = a long
**eeeee** sound). This will train your tongue to produce the correct
diphthong. - **Spelling Trick:** Remember: in German spelling, **"ei" =
\[ai\]** sound, **"ie" = \[i:\]** sound. A rhyme to help: *"E-I spricht
man* *AI; I-E spricht man* *EE."* Repeat this to yourself. It means "ei
is pronounced like *ai*, ie is pronounced like *ee*." - **Record &
Test:** Record yourself saying a simple sentence using both sounds, e.g.
*"Die* *Biene* *(bee) ist klein."* (Here **Biene** has *ie = ee* and
**klein** has *ei = eye*). Also try: *"Mein Tee ist hei√ü."* (my tea is
hot) -- *mein* (eye), *Tee* (long e), *hei√ü* (eye). Play it back and
check: does **"hei√ü"** sound different from **"Tee"**? If you find
you're mixing them up, isolate the words again and practice slowly. You
might also have a friend or app listen -- can they tell *"mein"* from
*"Mien(e)"*? Keep at it until the distinction is clear.

**Day 9:** *Diphthongs Part 2 -- AU & EU/√ÑU* -- **Pronouncing "au" (like
"ow") and "eu/√§u" (like "oy")**\

*"ow-toh"* for Auto). Repeat *Auto, Haus, Bauch* (belly) slowly, then at

**u**. - **"EU/√ÑU" as in "euch" or "H√§user":** Both **eu** and **√§u** in
German are pronounced like English *"oy"* (IPA \[…î è\]). For example,
**"neu"** (new) sounds like "noy", and **"H√§user"** (houses) sounds like
**HOY**-zer. Play **"Europa"**
(Europe)[\[17\]](https://talkpal.ai/mastering-german-intonation-tips-for-natural-speech-and-fluency/#:~:text=Intonation%20in%20Yes%2FNo%20Questions)
or **"euch"** (you, plural informal) on Forvo to get the sound in your
ear. Repeat words: *neu, Europa, B√§ume* (trees -- **√§u** in the middle),
*fr√§ulein* (old term for Miss -- **√§u** = *"froy"*). Concentrate on
rounding your lips for the **"oy"** sound. - **Practice in Pairs:** Mix
**au** vs **eu** for practice: say **"Paul trinkt Apfelsaft."** (Paul
drinks apple juice -- *Paul* with **au** like *"Pow-l"*) versus **"Paul
l√§uft nach Hause."** (*l√§uft* has **√§u** sound \[oi\], *Hause* has au
\[ow\]*). Another:* *"Das ist* *auch* *so."* *(that is also so --* auch
*= ow-ch) vs* *"Das* *Auto* *ist neu."* *(*Auto *= ow-toh,* neu *= noy).
These little phrase pairs let you toggle between the sounds. Listen to a
native if possible (Easy German might have sentences with* auch *and*
neu*). -* *Record & Mouth Check:* *Record yourself pronouncing a few*
*au* *and* *eu* *words (e.g. "Raum und Freude" --* raum *\[rowm\] =
room,* Freude *\[froy-de\] = joy). When listening back, ensure*
*"Freude"* *has that distinct* "froy" *sound. If your* *eu/√§u* *is
coming out too much like* *au* *or vice versa, adjust your mouth:* *au*
*begins with jaw more open (for "ah") and* *eu*\* begins more mid-mouth

**Day 10:** *Final Devoicing Intro* -- **B, D, G become P, T, K at Word
Ends**\
- **Key Rule -- "Auslautverh√§rtung":** In German, voiced consonants **B,
D, G** at the **end of a word** are *devoiced* -- pronounced as **P, T,
K**
respectively[\[18\]](https://talkpal.ai/master-easy-german-pronunciation-speak-like-a-native-fast/#:~:text=learners%20sound%20more%20natural%3A).
This is called final devoicing. For example, **"Tag"** (day) is
pronounced **\[tak\]**, not
\[tag\][\[18\]](https://talkpal.ai/master-easy-german-pronunciation-speak-like-a-native-fast/#:~:text=learners%20sound%20more%20natural%3A).
*"Hund"* (dog) sounds like **\[hunt\]**, *"ab"* (off) sounds like
**\[ap\]**. - **Listen to Examples:** Use Forvo to hear pairs like
**"Rad"** (wheel) and **"Rat"** (advice). Both will sound like **"Rat"
\[raht\]** due to final devoicing -- the *d* in *Rad* is pronounced as a
/t/. Listen to **"Hund"** and **"Hand"** -- *Hund* ends with /t/ (even
though spelled d) and *Hand* is also /hant/. Notice there is no vocal
cord vibration on those final consonants. - **Minimal Pairs to Try:**
Pronounce these aloud, paying attention to the end sound: **"bunt"**
(colorful) vs **"Bund"** (federation) -- both end in \[bunt\]; **"Tod"**
(death) vs **"Tot"** (dead) -- both sound like \[toht\]; **"Grab"**
(grave) vs **"Krabbe"** (crab, just to contrast a B in middle vs end) --
*Grab* ends with a \[p\] sound. It feels strange, but it's correct in
German! - **Drill it:** Make a quick list of words ending in b, d, g
(e.g. **"lieb"** (dear), **"Bad"** (bath), **"Tag"** (day), **"Zug"**

**Day 15:** *The "CH" Sound* -- **Distinguishing "ich" \[√ß\] vs "ach"

- **Two CH's:** German has two main **"ch"**

**soft, palatal \[√ß\]** -- like a gentle hissing (somewhere between an
English "h" and "sh"). This is the sound in **"ich"** (I) and **"mich"**
(me). After back vowels (a, o, u, au), "ch" is a **hard, throaty \[x\]**
-- similar to the **"ch" in Scottish "Loch"** or Spanish "j". This
appears in **"Bach"**, **"doch"** (though), **"Buch"**
(book)[\[21\]](https://talkpal.ai/master-easy-german-pronunciation-speak-like-a-native-fast/#:~:text=Beginners%20often%20stumble%20on%20specific,that%20don%E2%80%99t%20exist%20in%20English). -
**Mouth Position:** For **soft \[√ß\]**, spread your lips slightly (as if
starting to smile), place your tongue close to the roof of your mouth,
and push air over it -- it should sound like a soft hiss (like a cat

from your throat, like a subtle growl or clearing your throat
*lightly*. - **Listen & Repeat:** Use Forvo to hear **"ich"**,
**"dich"**, **"Milch"** (milk) -- note the soft, almost whistling
quality[\[21\]](https://talkpal.ai/master-easy-german-pronunciation-speak-like-a-native-fast/#:~:text=Beginners%20often%20stumble%20on%20specific,that%20don%E2%80%99t%20exist%20in%20English).

forceful, hoarser sound. Now practice minimal pairs where possible:
*"manch"* (some -- pronounced with \[√ß\] because *a* in *manch* is
followed by *ch* in same syllable after n) vs *"Mann"* + *"ich"* (two
words "Mann ich" to force a \[√ß\] after a). Or simply contrast *"ich"*
vs *"ach"*, *"Licht"* (light, \[√ß\]) vs *"Bucht"* (bay, \[x\]).
Exaggerate the difference when you say them. - **Common Pitfall:** Many
beginners pronounce **ich \[√ß\]** like English "ish" or "ik". Make sure
you're not closing to a /k/ -- it's purely a fricative sound, no stop.
On the other hand, **ach \[x\]** should not be voiced or become a /k/
either -- it's like a sustained rough breath. Practice holding the
sound: *"Ichhhhh"* (should feel like a continuous airy sound) vs
*"Achhhhh"* (continuous but raspier). If you can hold it, you're doing
it right. - **Record & Self-Correct:** Record yourself saying: *"Ich
gehe auch noch ins Buchgesch√§ft."* This sentence has **ich \[√ß\]**,
**auch \[x\]**, **Buch- \[x\]**. Play it back and listen: do your "ich"
and "auch/Buch" clearly sound

If not, adjust. It might help to consciously *smile* when saying ich (to
get \[√ß\]) and *open throat* when saying ach (\[x\]). This distinction

native ears, so it's great you're getting it down!

**Day 16:** *The German "R"* -- **Mastering the R sound (or acceptable

**Day 22:** *Statement vs Question Intonation* -- **Mastering Pitch
Patterns**\
- **Declarative = Falling:** In German statements (declarative
sentences), the pitch usually **falls at the
end**[\[27\]](https://talkpal.ai/mastering-german-intonation-tips-for-natural-speech-and-fluency/#:~:text=In%20declarative%20sentences%2C%20German%20typically,contour%20signals%20completion%20and%20certainty).
For example, *"Ich komme aus Berlin."* will start at a normal pitch and
end lower on
*Berlin*[\[27\]](https://talkpal.ai/mastering-german-intonation-tips-for-natural-speech-and-fluency/#:~:text=In%20declarative%20sentences%2C%20German%20typically,contour%20signals%20completion%20and%20certainty).
This signals completeness. - **Yes/No Question = Rising:** For yes/no
questions (questions without a question word), the pitch **rises toward
the
end**[\[17\]](https://talkpal.ai/mastering-german-intonation-tips-for-natural-speech-and-fluency/#:~:text=Intonation%20in%20Yes%2FNo%20Questions).
E.g., *"Kommst du aus Berlin?"* -- your voice should go **up** on
*Berlin?*[\[17\]](https://talkpal.ai/mastering-german-intonation-tips-for-natural-speech-and-fluency/#:~:text=Intonation%20in%20Yes%2FNo%20Questions),
indicating you're asking. - **Practice Pair:** Take a simple sentence
and make it a statement and a yes/no question. For instance: **"Du bist
m√ºde."** (You are tired.) vs **"Bist du m√ºde?"** (Are you tired?). Say
the statement -- **"Du bist m√ºde."** -- in a neutral tone and let your
voice drop on *m√ºde.* Now say **"Bist du m√ºde?"** -- start a bit higher
and have your voice lift on *m√ºde?* as if unsure. Exaggerate the rise
and fall to train your ear. This contrast is key in German; a flat
intonation on a question might confuse
listeners[\[28\]](https://talkpal.ai/mastering-german-intonation-tips-for-natural-speech-and-fluency/#:~:text=,the%20meaning%20or%20reduce%20clarity). -
**WH- Question Intonation:** Questions with a question word (wer, was,
wo, etc.) often have a **falling intonation**, similar to
statements[\[29\]](https://talkpal.ai/mastering-german-intonation-tips-for-natural-speech-and-fluency/#:~:text=Intonation%20in%20Wh).
E.g., *"Wo kommst du her?"* (Where do you come from?) will usually fall
at the end, because the question word itself signals it's a question.
Practice one: **"Wie hei√üt du?"** (What's your name?) -- you can give a
slight rise on *hei√üt* but generally it doesn't rise as much as a yes/no
question, often it falls by the end of *du*. Try both ways and listen to
which sounds more natural (most likely the one with a slight fall). -
**Listen and Imitate:** Use an audio example to solidify this. For
instance, find a clip of someone asking **"Geht es dir gut?"** (Are you
doing well?) vs saying **"Es geht mir gut."** (I'm doing well.). Many
language apps or YouTube videos on German intonation illustrate this.
Shadow the speaker: mimic the melody exactly, not just the words. Feel
how your voice moves up or down. If you can't find an audio, record
yourself doing both and ensure there's a clear difference -- maybe even
have a friend listen and identify which is the question. Mastering this
pitch change will help your German *sound* German. - **Marking Stress:**
In each practice sentence, also be mindful of **sentence stress** --
typically the most important word (the focus) is stressed. In *"Du bist*
*m√ºde."*, *m√ºde* carries the main stress (especially if we're
emphasizing that). In the question *"Bist* *du* *m√ºde?"*, *du* might be
stressed if the question is specifically asking about *you*. This tends
to happen naturally, but it's good to be aware that content words get
more emphasis. Play around by saying a sentence and putting stress on
different words to see how meaning changes (e.g., *"Ich* *komme aus
Berlin"* vs *"Ich komme aus* *Berlin"* -- stressing *Ich* might imply
*I* (not someone else) come from Berlin). This awareness adds polish to
your pronunciation.

**Day 23:** *Emphasis & Rhythm in Sentences* -- **Sentence Stress and
Linking**\
- **Content vs Function Words:** German sentence rhythm, like English,
usually stresses **content words** (nouns, verbs, adjectives) more than
**function words** (articles, prepositions, auxiliary verbs). For
example, in *"Der alte Mann gibt dem kleinen Junge einen Ball."* (The
old man gives the little boy a ball), the stresses would likely be on
**alte**, **Mann**, **kleinen**, **Junge**, **Ball**, rather than on
*der, dem, einen*. Practice reading a sentence and consciously stressing
the big meaning words. It should feel a bit sing-songy -- that's okay. -
**Emphasizing a Word:** If you want to emphasize a particular word (to
correct or highlight), Germans often do it by making that word louder
and higher pitched. E.g., *"Ich habe* *zwei* *Schwestern, nicht drei."*
(I have two sisters, not three) -- you'd stress **zwei**. Try such
contrastive emphasis: *"Nicht* *du, sondern* *ich!"* (Not you, but me!)
-- stress *du* and *ich* strongly. This gives you practice in
controlling sentence stress deliberately. - **Linking Sounds:** German
doesn't connect words as fluidly as some languages (English often links
consonant to vowel), but there is still some linking. Final consonant of
one word often runs into the starting vowel of the next. E.g., *"ist
es"* can sound like *"is-tes"*, *"geht es"* like *"gehtes"*. And an *-r*
at end of word often sounds like it starts the next word if the next
begins with a vowel (because the -r often reduces to a schwa sound).
Practice saying: **"Das ist eine gute Idee."** Slowly first, then faster
-- notice *"gute Idee"* may blend (the *e* in *gute* and *Idee* might
merge a bit, and the *e* in *eine* might link to *gute*). The goal is
not to slur, but to make your speech flow rather than choppy
word-by-word. - **Pausing in Groups:** A tip for sounding natural is to
use **chunking** -- group words and slightly pause between thought
groups, not between every word. For example, instead of "Ich... komme...
aus... den... USA.", say "Ich komme \| aus den USA." in two
breaths/chunks. Try reading a complex sentence from a book or article by
chunking it at logical places (commas or where you'd naturally pause).
This will inadvertently make your intonation more native-like too, as
you'll naturally drop pitch at the end of a clause. - **Shadow for
Rhythm:** Take 1-2 sentences from an **Easy German** video or podcast
transcript (they often have everyday sentences). Listen and shadow them,
focusing on the **rhythm and
stress**[\[15\]](https://talkpal.ai/mastering-german-intonation-tips-for-natural-speech-and-fluency/#:~:text=1,Regularly),
not just individual sounds. For instance, a sentence like *"Morgen
fahren wir zum Supermarkt, weil wir einkaufen m√ºssen."* Note how
**"Morgen"** might be slightly emphasized (setting time), slight rise
and fall at comma, then **"Supermarkt"** likely stressed,
**"einkaufen"** stressed. By shadowing, you'll mimic these subtle
patterns without overthinking. Do it multiple times until you feel the
"beat" of the sentence in your delivery matches the native's. -
**Self-Check:** Finally, record yourself reading a short paragraph or
